Fragrance labels can make the category seem more exact than it is. Eau de parfum, eau de toilette and cologne are common terms for different product styles, but they do not promise that a scent will last for one fixed number of hours on every person.

## Think of concentration as a starting point

In broad terms, a perfume extract contains a higher proportion of fragrance materials than an eau de parfum, while an eau de toilette is often lighter. The formula, climate, fabric, application amount and individual perception all affect how the scent develops.

A lighter formula can be a better choice when you want a more airy experience. A richer formula can feel more present, but it may not suit every setting or every nose. Test the category that matches the atmosphere you want before assuming the strongest option is best.

## Read the development, not just the first spray

The opening can shift quickly as alcohol evaporates and different materials become more noticeable. Try a scent on clean skin or a blotter, then return to it after a short walk or a quiet hour. Notice whether it feels close to the body, whether it grows louder and whether the dry-down still feels appealing.

If you are testing several fragrances, use separate blotters and take breaks. Scent fatigue is normal; smelling more products in a row does not necessarily make the comparison more accurate.

## Keep comfort in the decision

Fragrance can be a pleasurable cosmetic choice, but it is optional. If a product irritates your skin, triggers discomfort or feels intrusive in shared spaces, stop using it or choose a different application method. Ingredient lists and labels are useful, especially for people who already know they react to particular materials.
