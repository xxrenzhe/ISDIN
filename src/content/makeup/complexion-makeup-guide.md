Complexion makeup is easier to use when it is treated as adjustment rather than correction. The aim can be a little more evenness, a little more warmth or a finish that helps the rest of a look feel intentional. It does not need to erase every natural change in the skin.

## Start with the job, not the label

Ask what you want the product to do on an ordinary day. A skin tint can soften contrast while leaving freckles and texture visible. A foundation usually gives more pigment and a more uniform result. A concealer is useful when there are only a few areas you want to address.

Coverage language is not standardized across every brand, so a “medium” formula can feel very different from one product to the next. Begin with a small amount in the center of the face, then look at the result in daylight before deciding whether another pass is useful.

## Match undertone before chasing perfection

Shade depth is only one part of a match. The underlying cast can read pink, golden, neutral, olive or a mix that shifts through the year. Test a small stripe where the face meets the neck and let it sit long enough to dry down. A formula that disappears at that transition is usually a better starting point than one that only looks convincing on the hand.

If a perfect match is unavailable, choose the formula that is closest in undertone and use a sheer application. A concentrated product can be adjusted with a small amount of moisturizer or mixed with a neighboring shade, provided the texture stays comfortable.

## Build in thin passes

Press or sweep on one light layer, then pause. Most heavy-looking base makeup comes from adding more before the first layer has settled. A small brush can offer precision around the nose; fingertips can keep a sheer formula connected to the skin; a sponge can soften an edge without adding much product.

Remove the product as carefully as you apply it, especially around the eyes and hairline. If a cosmetic repeatedly stings, burns or causes a persistent reaction, stop using it and consider speaking with a qualified clinician.
