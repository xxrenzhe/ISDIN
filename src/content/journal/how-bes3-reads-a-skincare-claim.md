A polished claim can be true, partly true or technically unrelated to the product being sold. BES3 uses a repeatable set of questions before turning a front-label promise into editorial language.

## 1. What exactly is being promised?

We rewrite the claim in plain language. "Supports radiant skin" is vague; "reduced the measured appearance of dark spots after twelve weeks" is specific. We note the outcome, population, timeframe and comparison.

## 2. Is the evidence about the ingredient or the finished product?

An ingredient may have a plausible mechanism or published research, but the final formula can use a different concentration, vehicle or application schedule. We do not automatically transfer an ingredient study's result to every product containing it.

Finished-product studies are more directly relevant. We still ask how many participants completed the study, whether there was a control, who assessed the outcome and whether results were statistically and visibly meaningful.

## 3. Who funded and ran the work?

Industry-funded research is not automatically invalid, but the relationship matters. A brand's unpublished consumer questionnaire is different from a peer-reviewed controlled trial. We label data-on-file and brand claims as such.

## 4. Does the language cross into treatment?

Cosmetic claims concern cleansing, beautifying or altering appearance. Claims to treat disease or affect body structure can place a product in drug territory under U.S. law. BES3 avoids turning cosmetic copy into medical advice.

## 5. What are the practical limits?

We consider irritation, packaging, stability, application amount, price per realistic use and the routine around the product. A result achieved under supervised conditions may not translate to hurried daily use.

## Our publication language

We use **well established** when multiple relevant sources and professional guidance align, **promising** when early evidence is useful but limited and **limited** when a claim rests mainly on mechanism or marketing material. Those are explanations, not numerical ingredient scores.

When commercial links appear, the evidence standard does not change. The relationship is disclosed beside the link, and the reader should be able to understand the conclusion without clicking it.
