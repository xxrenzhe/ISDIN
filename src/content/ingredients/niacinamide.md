Niacinamide is a form of vitamin B3 used in serums, moisturizers and cleansers. Research supports several potential benefits, including barrier support, reduced water loss, a more even-looking tone and moderation of oiliness. The ingredient is versatile, which is why it appears in so many formulas.

## More is not automatically more effective

Many published cosmetic studies use niacinamide around 2% to 5%. Products at 10% or higher are common, but the larger number does not guarantee a proportionally larger result. Higher-strength serums can sting, flush or feel tacky for some users.

Check whether niacinamide is already present in a moisturizer or sunscreen before adding a dedicated serum. A moderate amount across one formula may be easier than layering several high-percentage products.

## Pairing is usually straightforward

Niacinamide can generally share a routine with vitamin C, retinoids, exfoliating acids and sunscreen. Old claims that niacinamide and vitamin C always deactivate each other do not reflect typical modern cosmetic use.

The practical issue is cumulative irritation. If a niacinamide serum also contains acids, zinc salts or multiple botanical extracts, judge the complete formula rather than assuming every reaction comes from vitamin B3.

## A simple place to start

Use a moisturizer containing niacinamide once daily, or apply a dedicated 2% to 5% serum before moisturizer. Give the routine several weeks. If redness or itching persists, stop and reintroduce only after skin is comfortable.

Niacinamide is a supporting ingredient, not a replacement for acne medication, sun protection or an appropriate evaluation of persistent pigmentation.
