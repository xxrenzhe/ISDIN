Hyaluronic acid is a humectant: it helps bind water. In a topical serum or moisturizer, that can make the surface feel smoother and temporarily soften the look of fine dehydration lines. It is useful, but it does not work like an injectable filler and does not permanently replace lost facial volume.

## Molecular weight changes the story

Labels may mention high-, low- or multi-molecular-weight hyaluronic acid. Larger molecules mainly form a hydrating film near the surface; smaller fragments may interact differently with skin. Marketing often turns those distinctions into precise depth claims that a cosmetic label cannot prove on its own.

The overall vehicle matters more than a long list of molecular weights. Glycerin, panthenol, emollients and occlusives can make a formula more complete.

## Why a serum can still feel drying

A water-binding serum is not always enough to reduce water loss. In a dry environment, apply it to slightly damp skin and follow with a moisturizer containing emollient and occlusive ingredients. If skin feels tight after the serum dries, simplify to a well-formulated moisturizer instead of adding more layers.

## Who may like it

Hyaluronic acid is generally easy to combine with sunscreen, retinoids, vitamin C and exfoliants. It can be helpful when a treatment routine creates temporary dryness. Some formulas become sticky or pill under sunscreen, which is a product-design issue rather than evidence that the ingredient is harmful.

Expect improved hydration and surface comfort. Do not expect structural lifting, permanent wrinkle removal or a substitute for barrier-supportive care.
