Vitamin C is an antioxidant involved in collagen synthesis and skin defense. In topical skincare, the difficult question is not whether vitamin C matters biologically. It is whether a particular form remains stable, reaches skin effectively and stays comfortable enough for consistent use.

## Ascorbic acid and derivatives

L-ascorbic acid is the form with the most direct research, but it is unstable in water and commonly requires an acidic formula. That low pH can sting sensitive skin. Dark or air-restrictive packaging helps, but it cannot make an opened serum last indefinitely.

Derivatives such as sodium ascorbyl phosphate, magnesium ascorbyl phosphate, ascorbyl glucoside and oil-soluble tetrahexyldecyl ascorbate are designed for improved stability or a different skin feel. They must be converted or behave differently in skin, so evidence for one form cannot simply be assigned to all of them.

## Read the formula, not the percentage alone

A high percentage is not automatically more useful. Look for the named form, packaging, supporting antioxidants, usage instructions and a credible period-after-opening symbol. With ascorbic acid serums, significant darkening toward orange-brown can signal oxidation.

## Morning or evening?

Morning use pairs logically with sunscreen because antioxidants and ultraviolet filters work in different ways. Vitamin C does not replace sunscreen. Evening use is also acceptable if it is easier to tolerate or separates the serum from other actives.

Apply after cleansing and before moisturizer and sunscreen. If the formula pills, use less, allow it to settle or move it to a different time of day.

Sensitive skin may prefer a lower-strength ascorbic acid product, a derivative or use every other day. The best choice is the one that remains stable and comfortable through the full bottle.
