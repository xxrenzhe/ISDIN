Tranexamic acid began as a medicine used to control bleeding. In dermatology it has also been studied in oral, injected and topical forms for pigmentation concerns. A cosmetic serum is not interchangeable with medical treatment, and oral tranexamic acid carries systemic risks that require clinical supervision.

## What topical products are trying to do

Topical tranexamic acid is used to interrupt signaling involved in excess pigment production. Studies and clinical experience suggest potential for melasma and post-inflammatory discoloration, but formulas, concentrations and study methods vary. The evidence is promising rather than perfectly standardized.

This makes it a reasonable option for someone who wants a targeted, generally straightforward cosmetic active, not a guaranteed pigment eraser.

## Where it fits

A simple morning routine can be cleanser, tranexamic acid serum, moisturizer as needed and broad-spectrum sunscreen. At night, it can sit before moisturizer. Product directions take priority because supporting ingredients and vehicle can change how a serum feels.

Tranexamic acid is often combined with niacinamide, licorice derivatives, vitamin C or exfoliating acids. More brightening ingredients are not always better. If the formula already contains several actives, avoid immediately stacking another acid or retinoid.

## What determines success

Sun protection is essential. Ultraviolet exposure can deepen existing marks, and visible light can be relevant in melasma. Reducing irritation also matters because inflammation can leave new pigment, especially in deeper skin tones.

Evaluate a well-tolerated routine over at least eight to twelve weeks. Stop if the product causes persistent burning, swelling or a rash. A dermatologist can help distinguish melasma, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and other causes that may need different management.
